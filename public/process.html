Project Update (March 12, 2019 - March 19, 2019)


Mark measurements on all wooden parts Cut all components, drill holes, and hand cut dowels Begin construction of the first iteration of the physical key rack

Weekly Objectives

The Raspberry Pi will be connected to the motors through a L298N motor driver. The motor driver accepts an external 12V DC power supply adapted from a power outlet. The 5V output of the Raspberry Pi will be used to control the motor driver. Upon detecting pressure on the door mat, the blind will open if it is not already open. The rotational speed of the motors will be adjusted by modifying the pulse width modulation (PWM) such that the blind rolls as fast as possible without swinging. The motors will then rotate for a fixed time until the blind is completely open or closed.

Physical Component Progress

Monday (March 25): Made measurements for the wood boards and dowels and trimmed it to precision Tuesday (March 26): Drilled holes for the pegs, cut dowels, put all the pieces together to make note of adjustments we need to make Thursday (March 28): Glued together the side panels. bottom, and middle divide.

Challenges

Rainy weather brought about some limitations to us as to where we could work on the project Many of the simple tasks took a lot longer than expected, like taking precise measurements and cutting/sanding dowels. Sanding in particular took a fairly long time in the process, and for the future we could invest in buying our own dowels and accommodating our drills to store-bought ones. Our current process was to make all measurements first, then drill holes and cut the pieces, and lastly assembling all the pieces. However, many of the pieces weren't exactly aligned after we drilled all of them, and this issue could have been prevented if we checked the dimensions/tried assembling the pieces before we drilled each piece. We will end up not using all of the dowels/holes that we drilled. The dowels and wood glue were not strong enough to keep the bottom panels in place, as some of the holes were not aligned properly on the bottom. To make up for this wood screws were used, but one was slightly off and pierced through the side of the key rack. This can be covered up by a hood for the cord connecting to the pressure mat.

Electronics Project Update (April 1, 2019 - April 2, 2019)


Component Progress

Created working floor mat out of velostat, copper tape, cardboard, and vinyl Successfully implemented electrical circuits for five pressure sensing key dishes Programmed the Raspberry Pi to handle electrical input and output state changes

Next Steps

Implement and wire motors Solder wires to pressure sensors to match physical component dimensions Purchase carpet to hide electronic components of floor mat, improving aesthetic appeal Begin building the second model of the physical key rack after understanding the issues encountered during construction of the first model.

Challenges

The previous Raspberry Pi was defective, so the Chi Pledge Class purchased a brand new Raspberry Pi 3 Model B The Chi Pledge Class performed calculations to find appropriate resistors to allow consistent responses upon applying pressure The floor mat was registering touch inaccurately and was frequently sensing pressure when none was being applied. Ultimately, the Chi Pledge Class decided to purchase a pressure sensing floor mat as a backup while also attempting to build a second floor mat with a different design.

Project Update (April 3, 2019 - April 9, 2019)


Component Progress

The key dishes and gears have been 3D printed and are ready to be sanded and cleaned. The prints turned out fine, but because of the print resolution of the printer (0.6mm nozzle), there is a lot of refining to do.

Motors

The motors are set up and rotating. They will be attached to the key rack and pull on belts to roll up the key rack veil. There were issues with the gears as the belt continued to slip off of them so new gears with phalanges on the sides to help keep the belt are being 3D printed. In addition, new key racks were designed with higher walls as keys continued to slip off the dishes during testing.
